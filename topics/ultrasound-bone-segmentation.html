Ultrasound Bone Segmentation for Prosthesis Design

Last update: October 2025

Medical Imaging nnU-Net Ultrasound Segmentation

⚠️ Confidentiality Notice

Due to confidentiality agreements, I'm not sharing source code, detailed implementation specifics, or sensitive project data publicly. Instead, I'm providing a high-level overview of the methodology and preliminary results.

Project Background

Before a patient-specific prosthesis can be manufactured, the geometry of a residual limb has to be measured. Until now, this has mostly been done by hand by orthopedic technicians. In this project, I'm using ultrasound to simplify and standardize the measurement process.

My Goal

I'm developing an automated deep learning pipeline to accurately segment bone and tissue structures from ultrasound images of residual limbs, enabling precise and standardized prosthesis design.

Why nnU-Net v2?

I chose nnU-Net v2 as the segmentation framework because it's the gold standard for medical image segmentation:

  • Self-configuring - Automatically adapts to any dataset without manual tuning
  • State-of-the-art performance - Consistently achieves top results in medical imaging challenges
  • Handles ultrasound challenges - Robust to speckle noise, shadows, and variable contrast
  • Works with small datasets - Designed for medical imaging where data is limited

Training Data

What I Used

I trained the model on a combination of:

  • My own ultrasound images and labels - Custom-collected data from the project
  • UltraBones100k dataset - A large-scale publicly available dataset

Why UltraBones100k?

The UltraBones100k dataset is a breakthrough for ultrasound bone segmentation:

  • 100,000+ ultrasound images - Unprecedented scale for bone segmentation
  • Automated labeling - Novel annotation method eliminates manual labeling bottleneck
  • Diverse coverage - Multiple bone structures and imaging conditions

By combining UltraBones100k with my custom project data, I achieved robust generalization across different ultrasound devices and better handling of anatomical variations.

Potential Impact

I believe this work could:

  • Standardize prosthesis design - Reduce variability in manual measurements
  • Improve patient outcomes - More accurate limb geometry leads to better-fitting prostheses
  • Speed up production - Automated segmentation accelerates the design process
  • Enable remote processing - Ultrasound scans can be analyzed from anywhere
  • Lower costs - Less need for specialized technician time
