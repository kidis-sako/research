The Challenge

Creating a custom prosthesis starts with measuring a patient's residual limb. Traditionally, orthopedic technicians do this by hand. This is a time-consuming process and varies from person to person.

I'm working on a (hopefully) better approach: using ultrasound imaging combined with deep learning to automatically identify bone and tissue structures. The goal is to make prosthesis design faster, more accurate, more consistent, and cheaper.

nnU-Net

I chose nnU-Net v2 for this project. It's the gold standard for medical image segmentation, and here's why it works so well:

  • It automatically configures itself for any dataset, so no manual tuning needed
  • It consistently achieves top results in medical imaging competitions
  • It handles ultrasound's unique challenges like speckle noise and shadows
  • It works well even with limited training data

Training the Model

I trained the model using two sources:

  • My own ultrasound images collected for this project
  • The UltraBones100k dataset paper (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.03783) I contacted the authors who provided me access to this dataset with over 100,000 labeled ultrasound images

UltraBones100k was a game-changer. Its automated labeling method and diverse coverage of different bone structures gave the model a strong foundation. Combined with my project-specific data, this helped the model generalize well across different ultrasound devices and handle various anatomical variations.

Why This Matters

If this approach works at scale, it could make a real difference:

  • More consistent prosthesis designs (less variability from manual measurements)
  • Better patient outcomes (more accurate fits)
  • Faster turnaround times (automated vs. manual process)
  • Remote analysis capability (scans can be processed anywhere)
  • Lower costs (less specialized technician time needed)
